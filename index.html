Cantonese Swear Words

Understanding the cultural context, meanings, and appropriate usage of Cantonese profanity

About Cantonese Profanity

Cantonese, spoken in Guangdong province, Hong Kong, and Macau, has a rich and diverse vocabulary of swear words that reflect the culture, history, and social norms of its speakers.

Unlike English profanity which often revolves around sexual themes, Cantonese swear words frequently involve family members, animals, and supernatural beings. Understanding these terms provides insight into Cantonese culture and values.

This guide explores some common Cantonese swear words with their meanings, pronunciation, and cultural context.

Diu

/diu/

Literal Meaning: To have sexual intercourse

Usage: Used as a general expletive similar to "f*ck" in English. Often the first word in compound swear phrases.

Cultural Context: Part of the "Five Great Curses" in Cantonese. Considered very vulgar and offensive in most contexts.
High Severity

Gau

/gau/

Literal Meaning: Dog

Usage: Used to insult someone by comparing them to a dog, implying they are contemptible or of low status.

Cultural Context: In Chinese culture, being called a dog is a serious insult as dogs are traditionally viewed as lowly animals.
Medium Severity

Zhong

/zung/

Literal Meaning: To plant (as in farming)

Usage: Used to curse someone with misfortune or to wish that something bad happens to them.

Cultural Context: Reflects the agricultural roots of Chinese civilization. The idea is "planting" misfortune in someone's life.
Medium Severity

Hai

/hai/

Literal Meaning: Female reproductive organ

Usage: Used similarly to "c*nt" in English, but can be even more offensive in Cantonese.

Cultural Context: Considered one of the most offensive words in Cantonese. Part of the "Five Great Curses."
High Severity

Lan

/laan/

Literal Meaning: Male reproductive organ

Usage: Used to call someone stupid or incompetent, similar to "d*ckhead" in English.

Cultural Context: Another of the "Five Great Curses." Using this word shows extreme anger or contempt.
High Severity

Pok Gai

/pok gai/

Literal Meaning: Fall on the street

Usage: A curse wishing someone would die homeless on the streets.

Cultural Context: Reflects the fear of dying without a proper burial, which is important in Chinese culture.
Medium Severity

Cultural Insights

Cantonese profanity reveals much about Chinese cultural values and taboos. Understanding these can help avoid unintentional offense when interacting with Cantonese speakers.

Family Importance

Many Cantonese swear words involve family members, reflecting the central importance of family in Chinese culture.

Ancestral Worship

Insults involving ancestors are particularly offensive due to the tradition of ancestral worship in Chinese culture.

Supernatural Beliefs

Some curses invoke ghosts or supernatural beings, reflecting traditional Chinese folk beliefs.
